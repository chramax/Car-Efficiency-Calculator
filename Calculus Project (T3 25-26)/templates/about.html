About This Project

Through this project, we implemented two of the most important concepts in calculus: limits and derivatives.

We developed a mathematical model that determines the most fuel-efficient speed of a car by analyzing how fuel consumption changes with velocity.

$$ E(x) = \frac{x}{a + bx + cx^2} $$

In this function:

x = speed (km/h)
a = baseline fuel consumption
b = rolling resistance constant
c = aerodynamic drag constant

Application of Limits

By applying limits to the function, we analyze extreme cases:

$$ \lim_{x \to 0} E(x) = 0 $$ $$ \lim_{x \to \infty} E(x) = 0 $$

These results show that fuel efficiency approaches zero when the car drives extremely slowly or extremely fast. Therefore, maximum efficiency must exist somewhere between these extremes.

Application of Derivatives

To find the optimal speed, we differentiate the efficiency function and set the derivative equal to zero.

$$ E'(x) = 0 $$ $$ a - cx^2 = 0 $$ $$ x = \sqrt{\frac{a}{c}} $$

This critical point gives the speed that maximizes fuel efficiency. The model demonstrates how calculus can be applied to real-world engineering problems such as transportation efficiency and energy conservation.

Why This Matters

This project shows how mathematical modeling can translate physical principles like air resistance and rolling friction into a usable optimization tool. It connects theoretical calculus concepts to practical automotive performance, making mathematics both applicable and impactful.
